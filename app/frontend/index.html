About Carmichael Numbers

Carmichael numbers are composite numbers that satisfy a congruence relation typically associated with prime numbers in modular arithmetic. Fermat's Little Theorem states that if p is prime, then for any integer a, the number ap − a is an integer multiple of p. Carmichael numbers are composite numbers that share this property, passing a Fermat primality test to every base relatively prime to the number despite not being prime. The first seven Carmichael numbers were discovered by Czech mathematician Václav Šimerka in 1885, with the smallest being 561 (equal to 3 × 11 × 17). These numbers are relatively rare: there are 20,138,200 Carmichael numbers between 1 and 1021, yet they are infinite in number, as proven by Alford, Granville, and Pomerance in 1994.

Applications of Carmichael Numbers

Carmichael numbers are significant in cryptography and primality testing, where they reveal limitations of certain verification methods. The fact that Carmichael numbers constitute rare instances where the strict converse of Fermat's Little Theorem does not hold precludes using that theorem as an absolute test of primality. This makes tests based solely on Fermat's Little Theorem less effective than strong probable prime tests such as the Baillie-PSW primality test and the Miller-Rabin primality test, which are crucial for cryptographic systems like RSA encryption. Understanding Carmichael numbers has led to the development of more sophisticated primality testing algorithms that avoid being fooled by these pseudoprimes, making them essential to modern computational number theory and the security of cryptographic protocols.
